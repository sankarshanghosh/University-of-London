We're switching gears now to a final slightly
more advanced topic in procedural generation of graphics, which
involves fractals. A fractal is a rough or
fragmented geometric shape that can be split into parts, each of which is at
least approximately a reduced size copy of the whole. Here are some pretty fractals, both in 2D and 3D. Notice how the whole is
repeated in smaller copies. The term fractal was coined
by Benoit Mandelbrot, who first described the
phenomenon fractals in the natural world and
defined them mathematically. The word is from the
Latin word fractus, which means broken or fractured. The concepts behind fractals had been around for centuries, but it was not until
Mandelbrot that these great mathematical
masterpieces were discovered. Fractals can also
be found in nature, which is where Mandelbrot's
first research began. We see them in clouds, in mountains, in how
rivers are formed, on the surface of the moon, and on living organisms as well, from the leaves of plants to the way trees spread
their branches. Have a look at the fern, the archetypal leaving fractal, where its leaf is repeated in every smaller copies or the
stunning Romanesco broccoli, which is so mesmerizing
to observe. We also see this self
repetition in seashells, such as the one for the nautilus and we even see it
in the human body. Here's an image of blood vessels. Notice the fractal
nature of the system. Nature is complicated and it's often difficult to
describe its structures. Classical mathematics
is well-suited to study the world that
we have created, the structures we have built. The patterns that
naturally exist in nature such as mountain
ranges, trees, and clouds were outside the
world of mathematic until Benoit Mandelbrot introduced
his new geometry model. He suggested that in fact you can apply mathematics
to the structures. There is an order between
the seeming chaos. There are formula that describe the clouds and
flowers and plants. It's just that there are
different kind of formula and they provide a different
kind of geometry. The heart of it all is the
concept of self-similarity. With fractal geometry formula, we're suddenly able to describe the world in a lot more detail. It didn't take long for
the same techniques to be used creatively
in the film industry. Fractal design principles have completely transformed the
magic of special effects, and they are used everywhere where complexity of textures, surfaces or materials
is required.
